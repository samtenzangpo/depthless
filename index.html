The “Mind-Only” System as Bridge between Mind and Matter

A Semi-Scientific and Philosophical Defense

Abstract

The Buddhist “mind-only” (cittamātra) system is frequently dismissed as incompatible with Madhyamaka or as philosophically naïve. This paper argues that such dismissals are unwarranted. By examining the status of subjectivity, objectivity, and experience itself, it is shown that “mind-only” functions not as an ultimate metaphysical claim but as a superior conventional ontology. Properly understood, it is fully compatible with the Two Truths doctrine, satisfies Chandrakīrti’s criteria for conventional validity, and serves as an effective conceptual bridge between physical and mental phenomena, as well as between Buddhist philosophy and contemporary scientific research.

1. Subjectivity, Objectivity, and the Problem of Qualia

The concept of qualia—irreducibly subjective experience—is difficult to reconcile with a Buddhist philosophical framework. Higher schools of Buddhist tradition assert that objective reality is ultimately untenable, a conclusion reached either through valid reasoning or via direct meditative insight.

However, once the notion of objective reality is relinquished, the concept of subjective reality becomes equally problematic. In the absence of objectivity, the term “subjective” loses its referent. Subjectivity presupposes an object against which it is defined. If neither objectivity nor subjectivity can be coherently maintained, what remains is experience itself, irreducible to either category.

This residual “experience” cannot be meaningfully classified as either subjective or objective. It is simply given.

2. Experience Itself as an Axiom: The Justification for “Mind”

To label this irreducible experiential field as “mind” or “consciousness” is admittedly problematic. Such labeling does not explain its origin, structure, or relation to anything outside itself. It merely assigns a name to what is already present.

Yet this move is not philosophically irresponsible. On the contrary, it mirrors standard scientific practice. All formal systems ultimately rest upon axioms—entities or principles that are not derivable from prior premises but are nevertheless declared to be fundamental. The existence of experience cannot be denied; therefore, it is legitimate to treat it as a foundational postulate. We will call it therefore “mind”.

In this context, naming this fullness of experience “mind” or “consciousness” is not a metaphysical assertion about ultimate reality, but a conventional designation for the total field of all knowable phenomena—which constitutes, upon reflection, apart from the lack of inherent existence, mind only.

3. The Meaning and Scope of “Mind-Only”

It is undeniable that all possible objects of knowledge arise within this experiential field; therefore the expression “mind-only” is justified in a strictly conventional sense. It does not claim that mind is an ultimate substance, nor does it deny emptiness. Rather, it proposes a coherent conventional ontology in which all phenomena—mental and physical alike—are understood as manifestations within experience.

Importantly, this position does not contradict the Two Truths doctrine. “Mind-only” is explicitly situated within conventional truth, not ultimate truth. The claim is not that mind is ultimately real, but that it provides a more refined and internally consistent conventional framework than competing ontologies.

4. Compatibility with Chandrakīrti’s Criteria

Chandrakīrti (following Nāgārjuna) outlines three criteria for valid conventional truth:

  1. It is well known in the conventional world
  2. It is not refuted by conventional cognition
  3. It is not refuted by ultimate cognition

The mind-only system satisfies all three.

4.1 Not Refuted by Ultimate Cognition

Ultimate cognition apprehends emptiness alone; it does not engage with conventional ontologies. Since mind-only is not proposed as an ultimate truth, it cannot be refuted by ultimate cognition.

4.2 Not Refuted by Conventional Cognition

A valid conventional refutation of mind-only would require a standpoint outside of mind or cognition itself. Such a standpoint is incoherent. Any attempt at refutation necessarily occurs within cognition and thus presupposes what it seeks to deny. This mirrors insights formalized in Gödel’s well known incompleteness theorem: no sufficiently complex system can fully establish or refute itself from within.

4.3 Well Known in the World

Mind-only perspectives are deeply embedded in Buddhist theory and practice. The formless realms (ārūpyadhātu), formless absorptions, and yogic experiences described throughout both Hīnayāna and Mahāyāna sources all reflect ontologies in which experience is not grounded in material externality.

5. Mind-Only Across Buddhist Traditions

Even the Theravāda Abhidhamma contains numerous elements that gesture toward a mind-only type of ontology, though these references are not explicitly stated and often overlooked in favor of practical meditation techniques.

Historically, prominent Buddhist thinkers—including Dharmakīrti, Serlingpa, and Candragomin, among others—endorsed views closely aligned with mind-only. The claim that mind-only is marginal or unorthodox is therefore historically inaccurate.

6. Relationship to Madhyamaka

It is often claimed that mind-only is incompatible with Madhyamaka. While this may hold for some unsophisticated interpretations, it does not apply to the system when properly understood.

Mind-only can function as a superior preparatory and supportive conventional framework that leads naturally to Madhyamaka insight. Even after insight into emptiness, mind-only may remain one’s highest conventional understanding, fully integrated with ultimate truth.

7. Mind-Only as a Non-Dual Bridge

Properly understood, mind-only dissolves the rigid dualism between mental and physical phenomena. It treats nāma and rūpa not as fundamentally distinct substances but as expressions of a shared experiential basis.

In this sense, mind-only is neither idealism nor materialism. It is a non-dual framework that unifies cognition and appearance without reifying either.

8. Parallels with Contemporary Science

Modern physics—particularly quantum mechanics—has increasingly destabilized naïve notions of physical objectivity. When supplemented by cognitive science and phenomenology, striking parallels emerge between contemporary scientific models and mind-only perspectives.

For readers interested in these parallels, further exploration of alternative yet scientifically valid interpretations of quantum mechanics is recommended.

Time Based Cosmology of a Depth-less Species

Or a Species Whose Cognition Evolved Without Spatial Interpretations — rev 2

Abstract

This paper explores a speculative cognitive and cosmological framework in which an intelligent species evolves without depth perception or spatial interpretive priors. Instead of constructing the world through geometric extension, such a species organizes experience through temporal patterns, relational dynamics, and scale-sensitive differentiation.

We analyze the implications of such a cognitive architecture for scientific development, metaphysical assumptions, the understanding of physical phenomena, and the emergence (or non-emergence) of concepts such as mass, distance, objects, motion, and classical space.

We further compare this depth-less model with characteristics of pre-decohered quantum mechanics, arguing that the species’ cosmology would align closely with a time-based relational ontology similar to the universal wavefunction.

Critically, we argue that this alternative cognitive framework is not merely hypothetical but represents a viable evolutionary path that humans possess all necessary conditions to develop, either culturally or through future cognitive evolution. Furthermore, awareness of this alternative enables conscious toggling between spatial and depth-less modes of perception.

1. Introduction

Human cognition has evolved around depth perception, spatial navigation, and interaction with a three-dimensional environment. This has profoundly shaped our scientific models: classical mechanics, geometry, cosmology, and even intuitive notions of agency are fundamentally spatial.

But these commitments are not logically necessary. Visual perception itself is free to interpretation as “with depth component” or without depth—both are equally valid and change nothing of the retinal image itself. The depth interpretation is an overlay, not inherent to the sensory data.

It is possible to systematically analyze a species whose perceptual and cognitive apparatus never evolved this spatial interpretation. Such a species would not interpret the world in terms of “near/far,” “objects occupying space,” or “trajectories.” Instead, they would perceive temporal succession, pattern-variation, and relational coherences.

This paper describes how such a species might conceptualize the world and what kind of cosmology, physics, and scientific knowledge might emerge from this radically different cognitive foundation. Significantly, we argue that humans possess all necessary conditions to have evolved this way, or to evolve toward it culturally and cognitively in the future.

2. The Depth-less Cognitive Architecture

2.1 Absence of Depth Priors

In humans, spatial depth is not merely a perceptual feature—it is a cognitive organizing principle applied to sensory data that does not inherently encode depth. The two-dimensional retinal image contains patterns of variation: texture gradients, relative sizes, occlusions, and parallax effects over time.

Humans interpret these patterns as indicating depth and distance. A depth-less species simply never develops this interpretive overlay. They perceive the same rich, structured patterns but organize them as relational and temporal characteristics rather than as spatial arrangements.

2.2 Temporal and Relational Primacy

Because there is no interpretation of depth, all organization of experience defaults to temporal and relational structure. The world consists of coherently evolving pattern-clusters, transitions rather than trajectories, and cycles rather than distances. Patterns are characterized by their temporal signatures, phase relationships, and correlations with other patterns.

2.3 All Perception as Process

Critically, the depth-less interpretation is all-pervasive and includes the perceiving agent. There is no experience of “solid objects” because everything is understood as process.

What humans interpret as solid, bounded entities are, in this framework, highly stable, slowly-varying pattern-clusters with strong internal temporal coherence. The ideas of physical mass, spatial extension, or near and far sides of objects never arise.

2.4 Sensory Coordination Without Space

All sensory modalities must be conceptually coordinated to comply with and support the depth-less framework, and this is entirely possible because no human sense inherently encodes depth:

  • Touch and haptics are experienced not as contact with solid surfaces at locations, but as temporal patterns of pressure-variation, texture-rhythms, and resistance-dynamics. What we call shape becomes a characteristic temporal signature when motor-patterns are applied.
  • Proprioception is not “body position in space” but current state in the context of possible motor-pattern configurations. Movement is transition between motor-states, not displacement through space.
  • Sound is already largely temporal and relational in structure. Spatial localization of sound—in the absence of such prior intuition—would simply not be extracted. Sound remains pure temporal pattern.
  • Smell and taste are experienced as pure quality-patterns evolving over time, with no association to sources at locations.
  • Sensorimotor integration coordinates different pattern-types into a unified temporal model where correlations across modalities provide predictive power, without requiring spatial mediation.

2.5 Agency Without Geometry

Agency arises not from spatial orientation but from being a locus of directly modulated patterns. The agent is not a bounded body moving through space but a stable attractor in the stream of temporal activity—a subset of patterns that respond to intentions and have a distinctive self-caused character.

Action is experienced as: “I modulate my motor-patterns, and certain other pattern-clusters undergo correlated transformations.”

To briefly illustrate this it must be considered that even in our present everyday experience, what one normally considers “my own body”—mirrored or in its actuality—is merely a subset of patterns appearing within the visual field itself while no information of ownership being inherently encoded anywhere. The strongly felt sense of ownership is no more than conceptual overlay. However the mere conventional validity of such “ownership” is not disputed here. As such, it exists.

3. Scientific Development Without Space

3.1 Physics Without Solid Objects

Without spatial priors, the concept of solid “objects” does not arise. What humans call objects are, for this species, recurrent pattern-aggregates with persistence through time and characteristic modes of variation. Since everything is process, there are no “things” to track, only evolving dynamics.

3.2 No Notion of Distance or Mass

Since “near/far” does not exist, no quantity corresponding to distance emerges. Instead, they quantify rate of change, phase relations, correlation strength, and temporal lag.

Similarly, mass as “quantity of matter in a bounded object” never arises. Conservation laws would concern pattern-complexity, information content, or relational structure rather than material substance.

3.3 Gravity and Force as Temporal Correlation

Gravity becomes a systematic pattern of correlated transitions. What we call “falling” is, in their terms, a predictable shift in the temporal dynamics of one pattern-cluster relative to another. Forces are not pushes or pulls through space but descriptions of how pattern-transformations reliably correlate.

3.4 Progressive Scientific Development

Their science would likely develop through:

  • Pattern recognition and taxonomy: cataloguing recurrent pattern-types and their temporal signatures.
  • Dynamics and correlation laws: formulating rules about which transformations are compatible, discovering conservation principles of relational structure.
  • Multi-scale structure: discovering that increasing temporal resolution (zooming) reveals nested patterns at finer scales, with high-frequency oscillatory signatures at small scales.
  • Mathematics would emphasize differential equations, phase relationships, spectral analysis, and information theory, but not geometry or spatial coordinates.

4. Relational Cosmology

4.1 Time-Based Universe

Their cosmology is not geometric but chronological and relational. Instead of galaxies, stars, and planets, they describe long-period oscillatory structures, slow transformations, and resonant clusters. The universe is understood as a vast system of temporally evolving correlations at multiple scales.

4.2 Zooming Instead of Traveling

Exploration involves increasing the resolution of patterns rather than changing location. When they point their equivalent of a telescope at what we call a distant galaxy, they experience it as increasing the temporal and structural resolution of a particular pattern-cluster in their perceptual field, revealing finer-grained substructure with its own dynamics.

Indeed, even human telescopes phenomenologically do not transport us anywhere—they reveal more detail in patterns already present in our sensory field. The spatial narrative of “looking across vast distances” is interpretive.

4.3 No Geometric Questions (flat versus spherical Earth or otherwise)

Questions about the “shape” of the world, flat versus curved space, or spatial topology do not arise. Cosmological questions concern the global structure of temporal correlations, scales of periodicity, and emergence of stable pattern-configurations. For example, what we call a “sphere” would be perceived as a coherent recognisable circular pattern exhibiting periodic complexity.

5. Parallels with Quantum Mechanics

5.1 Pre-Decohered World as Depth-less Universe

From our perspective, the depth-less species’ worldview resembles the quantum wavefunction before decoherence: no geometric interpretation, no definite objects, no spatial localization, only evolving correlations and superposed amplitudes.

For them, this would simply be called “the World” or “Reality” without reference to quantum mechanics, which is our framework for understanding the mismatch between fundamental physics and our spatial intuitions.

5.2 Wavefunction as Pattern Multiverse

The wavefunction is not a spatial wave but a high-dimensional structure encoding correlations at every scale. This mirrors their cosmology, where structure is relational rather than geometric, and complexity exists at all levels of temporal resolution.

5.3 Measurement as Temporal Clarification

Without spatial ontology, what we call wavefunction collapse is not localization in space but a transition from ambiguous relational patterns to clearer, more stable ones—a shift in temporal dynamics rather than spatial determination.

6. Emergence of Selfhood Without Space

6.1 The Agent as Temporal Coherence

Without confining spatial boundaries their concept of selfhood would necessarily differ from ours. “I” would not be perceived as a bounded entity located “here,” but as a coherent attractor in pattern-dynamics—what represents “me” is continuity and self-similarity of certain patterns over time.

The boundary between self and other becomes merely functional, not spatial: “patterns I can directly modulate” versus “patterns I can only observe or indirectly influence”.

6.2 Weaker Self-Other Distinction

Other agents are viewed as not spatially separated beings but parallel temporal processes. The distinction resembles different voices in a choir rather than separate objects in space. Communication and interaction are naturally experienced as resonance or phase-coupling rather than signal transmission across distance.

6.3 No Interior-Exterior Divide

Without spatial containment, there is no obvious basis for private versus public experience. Thoughts might not feel “hidden inside” but rather like subtle pattern-modulations that others have less direct access to. Privacy becomes resolution-limited observation rather than spatial inaccessibility.

6.4 Death as Decoherence

Death changes from “my body ceasing to exist in space” to “my characteristic pattern-signature decoheres, loses stability, dissolves back into the general dynamics”—a melody ending rather than an object being destroyed.

6.5 Social Organization Without Property

Even in human experience, property ownership is entirely conceptual and social—fences do not contain ownership, deeds do not encode natural properties of land.

The depth-less species would see this clearly, as there is no temptation to reify property as spatial fact. They would recognize all such distinctions as coordination agreements about pattern-access rights and negotiated boundaries in the space of permitted actions.

They might not develop property concepts at all, or might develop very different ones based on temporal access rights or contribution to pattern-maintenance.

7. Why Humans Did Not Evolve This Way—Yet We Could Have

Spatial cognition as default possibly offered evolutionary advantages for early humans: navigation, tool manipulation, hunting, and survival in a terrestrial environment. However, this does not mean spatial interpretation was inevitable or optimal in all contexts.

Humans possess all necessary conditions to have evolved depth-less cognition:

  • The raw sensory data does not determine spatial interpretation—retinal images are two-dimensional, haptic sensations are temporal, proprioception is state-based.
  • Depth perception is learned and fragile—individuals who gain sight after congenital blindness struggle with depth; optical illusions reveal depth is inferred.
  • Meditative and contemplative traditions have long recognized that objects are processes and that spatial interpretation is constructed.
  • Modern physics is already revealing the depth-less structure of fundamental reality through quantum mechanics and field theory.

This is not merely hypothetical. The alternative mode exists, and once one becomes aware and convinced of its validity, one is able to switch between spatial and depth-less modes of perception at will, using whichever is more helpful at the moment.

This requires deep contemplation to understand that spatial interpretation is added rather than inherent, followed by conviction that the depth-less framework is genuinely valid, and then familiarization through practice in perceiving without spatial overlay.

8. Future Evolution and Cultural Emergence

Since the door to depth-less cognition is open—or can be opened—the same realization might occur on a large scale, naturally.

This could happen through:

  • Recognition spreading that spatial interpretation is optional.
  • Practices developing that cultivate depth-less perception.
  • Cultural frameworks emerging that normalize and systematize this view.
  • Developmental pathways shifting in learning environments emphasizing temporal and relational organization.

Conditions favoring this shift include:

  • Digital environments where spatial metaphors are arbitrary
  • Technological mediation decoupling perception from embodiment
  • Scientific understanding undermining naive spatial realism
  • Cultural or ecological pressures favoring less object-based, boundary-based, or territory-based thinking

This idea has not emerged from a single source but has naturally formed from a large field of contributing conditions, including quantum mechanics, process philosophy, neuroscience, contemplative traditions, and the emergence of AI language models that provide new cognitive scaffolding for exploring and articulating complex frameworks outside conventional spatial thinking.

9. Discussion

This cognitive architecture yields a coherent experiential world and viable scientific methodology. Its cosmology is temporally structured, relational, and rich in complexity at all scales. The framework is not science fiction but a genuine alternative that humans could adopt, either individually through perceptual training or collectively through cultural evolution.

The depth-less species does not experience an impoverished or limited world—they experience the same richness and variety of structure humans do, but organized through different principles. Their science would be internally consistent and predictive. Their technology would manipulate patterns through temporal coordination rather than spatial force.

Most significantly, their worldview aligns naturally with the fundamental structure revealed by quantum mechanics, suggesting that space may not be fundamental but rather an evolved interpretation overlaying a deeper relational reality.

10. Conclusion

A species with no spatial priors would form a time-based relational science. Their worldview parallels quantum mechanics before classicality emerges through decoherence. This suggests that space may not be fundamental but an evolved interpretation of underlying temporal and relational dynamics.

Humans possess all necessary conditions to perceive and organize experience in this way. The depth-less framework is not hypothetical but available. With awareness, contemplation, and practice, individuals can toggle between spatial and depth-less modes, gaining cognitive flexibility and access to alternative ways of understanding reality, selfhood, and the nature of existence itself.

The question is not whether this alternative is possible, but whether and how humanity will come to recognize and cultivate it.
